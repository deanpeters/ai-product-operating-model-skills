# Worked Example: Professional Learning Platform

**Synthetic example.** The organization, evidence, and decisions below are fictional.

## Context

A mid-sized professional learning platform has six disconnected AI pilots: course summarization, a learner chatbot, instructor content generation, sales-email drafting, support automation, and an adaptive practice coach. Leaders describe the strategy as “AI in every learning journey.”

The decision is which arena should receive the next two quarters of product investment.

## Evidence and Assumptions

| Claim | Evidence | Limitation | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Learners struggle to apply concepts after courses | Interviews and follow-up surveys across three programs | Self-reported behavior; limited workplace observation | Partially supported |
| Guided practice could improve application | Small facilitated pilot improved rubric scores | 42 learners and human coaching, not AI | Partially supported |
| The platform has distinctive assessment content | Ten years of instructor-authored rubrics and examples | Rights and reuse permissions vary | Supported with constraint |
| An AI practice coach will improve renewal | No direct evidence | Causal link has not been tested | Assumption |
| Generic content generation is strategically differentiating | Competitors offer similar tools | Weak evidence of differentiation | Not supported |

The organization’s current strategic maturity remains `2 — Emerging`: evidence exists, but no repeatable thesis has yet changed portfolio decisions.

## Strategic Choices

| Choice | Decision |
|---|---|
| Arena | Post-course practice and feedback for learners in role-based programs |
| Customer outcome | Learners apply target skills with less dependence on live instructor time |
| Product outcome | Increased completion of practice cycles and demonstrated rubric improvement |
| Economic hypothesis | Higher program renewal and scalable coaching capacity |
| AI role | Generate bounded practice variants and formative feedback grounded in approved rubrics |
| Differentiating advantage | Proprietary, instructor-tested rubrics and feedback examples with permission controls |
| Required conditions | Rights mapping, representative evaluation, instructor review, and learner escalation path |

## Boundaries and Non-Goals

- Do not fund generic course-summary features as a strategic differentiator.
- Do not let AI make certification or employment-readiness decisions.
- Do not scale instructor-content generation until rights and provenance controls exist.
- Do not claim renewal impact until the causal chain is tested.

## Strategy Thesis

> We will focus AI product investment on guided post-course practice for professionals in role-based programs because current evidence shows a gap between course completion and applied performance. We believe bounded practice generation and rubric-grounded formative feedback can increase meaningful practice cycles and demonstrated skill application, which may improve renewal and coaching capacity when rights, evaluation, instructor oversight, and learner escalation are operational. We will differentiate through our instructor-tested rubrics and feedback examples. We will not pursue generic content generation as a primary differentiator or delegate certification decisions to AI. Over the next two quarters, we will test learner behavior, feedback quality, instructor workload, and renewal leading indicators, and we will revise or stop if representative evaluations fail or learners do not complete more useful practice.

## Portfolio Implications

| Initiative | Decision | Owner | Evidence expected |
|---|---|---|---|
| Adaptive practice coach | Fund bounded discovery and pilot | Product director | Practice completion, rubric improvement, feedback-quality evaluation |
| Course summarization | Maintain, do not expand | Existing Product Manager | Usage and support evidence only |
| Generic instructor generation | Pause | Content leader | Rights map and demonstrated instructor value |
| Sales-email drafting | Move outside strategic product portfolio | Revenue operations | Workflow efficiency evidence |

## Next Learning Bets

1. Run a six-week pilot in two programs comparing practice completion and rubric improvement with current support.
2. Build a rights and authoritative-source map for rubrics and feedback examples.
3. Evaluate feedback across common cases, edge cases, and potentially harmful overconfidence.
4. Interview instructors about whether review workload is sustainable.

The product strategy leader owns the investment decision. The thesis will be reviewed after the pilot or immediately after a material evaluation, rights, or learner-safety issue.
