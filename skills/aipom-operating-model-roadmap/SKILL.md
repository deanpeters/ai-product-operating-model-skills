# AIPOM Operating Model Roadmap

## What Is It

Convert evidence-based findings into a sequenced operating-model change plan. The roadmap connects consequential conditions to interventions, dependencies, owners, evidence milestones, decisions, and review cadence across 30, 90, 180, and 365 days.

## Why Use It

Assessments fail when every gap becomes a workstream, low scores determine priority mechanically, or a transformation plan outruns ownership and learning capacity. This workflow builds the smallest coherent sequence that can change practice and generate evidence.

## When to Use It

Use after the AIPOM assessment or a comparable evidence-based diagnosis. Refresh after major evidence, incidents, strategic changes, or completed roadmap horizons.

## What It Produces

- Priority conditions and outcome logic
- Critical safeguards and constrained decisions
- Intervention and dependency sequence
- 30-, 90-, 180-, and 365-day roadmap
- Owners, evidence milestones, decision gates, and review cadence
- Assumptions, unresolved questions, and deferred work

## Who Should Participate

Include the accountable executive, Product Operations, portfolio and product leaders, technical and data leaders, governance partners, finance or operations, and owners expected to deliver the first 90 days.

## Evidence to Bring

Bring assessment profiles, evidence confidence, disagreements, critical gaps, current initiatives, strategic priorities, capacity, dependencies, existing change programs, and examples of previous adoption or implementation failure.

## How to Do It

1. **Load the assessment ledger.** Preserve scope, evidence, assumptions, disagreements, critical gaps, and recommendations.
2. **Confirm the decision horizon.** Name what must improve and which decisions the roadmap must enable.
3. **Prioritize conditions.** Consider consequence, dependency, strategic relevance, evidence, tractability, and ownership—not score alone.
4. **Apply safeguards first.** Sequence immediate constraints for critical governance, safety, legal, privacy, security, evaluation, or accountability gaps.
5. **Map dependencies.** Identify which strategy, portfolio, workflow, context, evaluation, governance, or capability conditions unlock others.
6. **Select interventions.** Use the smallest AIPOM skills or foundational methods that can change each condition.
7. **Sequence horizons.** Put containment and evidence in 30 days, repeatable practice in 90, operational integration in 180, and measured reuse or scaling in 365.
8. **Define milestones as evidence.** Name decisions, behaviors, outcomes, and proof of use—not document completion alone.
9. **Assign ownership and governance.** Name one human owner per decision, contributors, escalation, cadence, and stop or revision triggers.
10. **Check load and coherence.** Defer work that exceeds capacity or does not support the chosen outcome.

## Facilitation Protocol

### Guided mode

Ask one question at a time, beginning with the decision and most consequential condition. Present numbered sequencing options and explain dependencies and tradeoffs.

### Context-dump mode

Extract findings, existing work, owners, capacity, and dependencies first. Draft a provisional roadmap and ask only about gaps that could change sequence or ownership.

### Best-guess mode

Label assumptions, reduce scope, prioritize evidence-producing work, and avoid claiming organizational commitments without owners. Preserve all unresolved critical gaps.

For multiple sessions, maintain the assessment context ledger plus roadmap decisions, changes, owners, and deferred work.

## Decision Logic

- If a critical gap constrains launch, scale, autonomy, or sensitive-data use, contain it in the first horizon.
- If several gaps share a root cause, sequence the root intervention first.
- If strategy is unclear, avoid scaling a broad portfolio; establish thesis and bet discipline.
- If sources or behavior are untrusted, resolve context and evaluation before autonomy.
- If practices work locally, build reuse and stewardship before enterprise standardization.
- If capacity is weak, select fewer interventions with clear owners rather than parallel theater.
- If evidence is absent, make the early roadmap an evidence-building plan and cap maturity expectations.

Use four intervention postures:

1. **Contain:** stop or limit an exposed action.
2. **Learn:** reduce a consequential uncertainty.
3. **Establish:** create a repeatable owned practice.
4. **Scale:** standardize only after evidence of use and outcome.

## Completion Criteria

Finish with evidence-backed priorities, critical safeguards, dependency logic, horizon milestones, named owners, measures, decision gates, review cadence, assumptions, unresolved questions, and explicitly deferred work.

## Key Concepts

- A roadmap sequences organizational change, not skill production.
- Milestones describe evidence and decisions, not completed documents.
- Dependencies matter more than filling every category evenly.
- Smaller owned motions outperform large unowned programs.
- Roadmaps are revised when evidence changes.

## Organizational Applications

Use for enterprise baselines, business-unit improvement, post-incident recovery, portfolio governance, capability programs, and operating-model design sprints.

## Common Pitfalls

- Turning all assessment gaps into simultaneous workstreams
- Prioritizing by lowest score alone
- Using artifact delivery as the maturity milestone
- Assigning committees instead of accountable owners
- Scaling before evidence of repeatable use
- Ignoring current programs, capacity, and change fatigue
- Creating a one-year plan with no 30-day decision

## Combine With

Use the assessment as the evidence source and category advisors to deepen uncertain diagnoses. Invoke component skills only where they support the chosen sequence and outcome.
