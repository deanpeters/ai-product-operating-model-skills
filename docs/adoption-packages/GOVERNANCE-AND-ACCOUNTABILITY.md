# Executive Adoption Package: Governance and Accountability

Use this package to translate responsible-AI principles into usable ownership, decision rights, autonomy boundaries, controls, escalation, incident response, and trust evidence.

## Sponsor Decision

> Which AI actions are permitted, which require human approval, which are prohibited, who is accountable, and how will the organization detect, contain, recover from, and learn from failure?

The package turns policy into operating practice. Completing it does not establish legal or regulatory compliance.

## Best Fit

- Ownership is spread across committees or implied by participation
- “Human in the loop” does not define authority, criteria, or capacity
- AI or agent autonomy is increasing
- Product teams cannot translate policy into launch and operating decisions
- Escalation, rollback, incident response, or trust evidence is weak
- Governance is experienced as late approval rather than decision support

## Sponsor and Participants

The sponsor is normally the executive accountable for the AI product, operating model, or risk posture. Include product, engineering, data, operations, legal, privacy, security, safety, risk, compliance, AI governance, incident response, and affected-domain representation proportionate to scope.

## Evidence to Prepare

- Decisions, actions, populations, environments, and consequences in scope
- Current policies, standards, contractual duties, and approval paths
- Proposed AI actions and existing human review
- Behavior, evaluation, production, incident, and recovery evidence
- Existing accountability, escalation, exception, and audit records
- Relevant data, context, vendor, jurisdiction, and autonomy constraints

## Indicative Engagement

**Planning range:** 5–9 facilitated hours across 3–5 sessions, plus an incident or escalation exercise where needed.

1. **Diagnose the consequential gap:** use the [Responsible AIPOM Governance Advisor](../../skills/responsible-aipom-governance-advisor/SKILL.md) to select the next control motion rather than launching a generic governance program.
2. **Assign accountability:** use the [AIPOM Accountability Charter](../../skills/aipom-accountability-charter/SKILL.md) to define decision rights, evidence duties, review, contribution, escalation, and exceptions.
3. **Bound authority:** use the [AIPOM Autonomy Boundary Designer](../../skills/aipom-autonomy-boundary-designer/SKILL.md) to classify independent, approval-required, and prohibited actions with evidence needed for change.
4. **Prepare for failure:** use the [AIPOM Risk, Control, and Incident Playbook](../../skills/aipom-risk-control-incident-playbook/SKILL.md) to design prevention, detection, containment, rollback, communication, investigation, remediation, and learning.
5. **Exercise the system:** test a representative incident, escalation, or authority dispute; revise roles and controls based on observed performance.
6. **Communicate evidence when earned:** use the [AIPOM Trust Assurance Pack Builder](../../skills/aipom-trust-assurance-pack-builder/SKILL.md) only when current evaluation and production evidence supports the claims.

## Package Outputs

- Governance posture and critical-gap diagnosis
- Accountability charter and decision-rights matrix
- Autonomy boundary matrix and evidence-change rules
- Escalation, exception, stop, and rollback policy
- Risk-control and incident-response playbook
- Exercise findings, owners, and remediation actions
- Audience-appropriate trust evidence when supported

## Exit Conditions

The package is complete when consequential decisions and actions have named human authorities, operating controls and escalation paths are usable, incident roles have been exercised where warranted, and unresolved specialist decisions remain visible. A published policy or committee charter is not enough.

## Authority and Caveats

The package coordinates product and governance practice; it does not provide legal advice, certify compliance, or transfer accountability to AI or a facilitator. Required specialists make decisions within their authority. Missing approval remains a constraint, and high scores elsewhere cannot compensate for a critical control gap.
