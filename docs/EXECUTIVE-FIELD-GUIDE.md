# Executive Field Guide to the AI Product Operating Model

## The Leadership Problem

Many organizations already have AI pilots, copilots, prototypes, vendor experiments, governance committees, and training programs. What they often lack is a connected way to decide which opportunities matter, what evidence is sufficient, how teams should work differently, where human authority remains mandatory, and when an initiative should scale, change, pause, or stop.

That gap is an operating-model problem. More AI activity will not solve it by itself.

An AI product operating model is the connected system an organization uses to choose AI opportunities, allocate investment, organize product-team work, supply trusted context and data, evaluate behavior, govern accountability, and improve capability over time.

AIPOM is a practical library for improving that system one consequential decision and repeatable practice at a time.

## The Decisions It Should Improve

An effective AI product operating model helps leaders and product teams decide:

- Which AI opportunities support customer and economic outcomes
- Which bets should be explored, funded, constrained, scaled, paused, pivoted, or retired
- What evidence is required before a material commitment or launch
- How human and AI responsibilities should be divided in recurring work
- Which sources, data, and context can be trusted and used
- What acceptable AI behavior means and how it will be evaluated
- Where human review, approval, escalation, and accountability remain mandatory
- How effective local practices become reusable organizational capability

The objective is not a maturity score, a completed canvas, or a larger catalog of AI initiatives. It is better decisions, shorter learning cycles, clearer accountability, stronger evidence, and repeatable organizational practice.

## Four Levels of Leadership Attention

| Level | Leadership question |
|---|---|
| Organization | Are strategy, governance, capability, and operating systems working together? |
| Portfolio | Are investment, evidence, dependencies, and risk being managed across initiatives? |
| Product team | Are recurring decisions, workflows, context, and human-AI responsibilities improving? |
| Initiative | Is this specific AI bet valuable, ready, controlled, and accountably owned? |

These levels are related but not interchangeable. A well-run pilot does not prove organizational maturity. An enterprise policy does not prove that product teams use it. A portfolio total cannot average away a critical initiative-level safety or accountability gap.

## Seven Connected Conditions

AIPOM examines seven conditions that need to reinforce one another:

1. **Strategy and Economic Outcomes** — where AI creates meaningful value and which outcomes should change.
2. **Portfolio and Investment Choices** — which opportunities deserve exploration, funding, scale, constraint, or retirement.
3. **Product-Team Workflows** — how decisions, evidence, collaboration, and human-AI responsibilities work in practice.
4. **Context, Knowledge, and Data** — which sources and data can be trusted, used, refreshed, retained, or excluded.
5. **Evaluation and Evidence** — what acceptable behavior means, how it is tested, and what evidence supports a decision.
6. **Governance and Accountability** — who owns decisions, which controls apply, and what triggers review, escalation, or recovery.
7. **Capability, Adoption, and Reuse** — how people learn through applied work and how effective practices become reusable.

The categories are diagnostic lenses, not seven separate transformation programs.

## How to Begin

Do not begin by deploying all 41 skills or commissioning an enterprise-wide maturity exercise by default. Begin with one consequential decision whose outcome, evidence, ownership, and next action matter.

### Start with product direction

Choose [Set AI Product Direction](journeys/SET-AI-PRODUCT-DIRECTION.md) when the Head of Product, CPO, SVP of Product, or equivalent product leader needs to turn scattered AI activity into explicit strategic choices and a portfolio posture.

**Leadership result:** a product strategy thesis, portfolio posture, stated non-goals, evidence gaps, decision rights, and the next bets or stops.

### Start with the technical readiness bar

Choose [Establish the AI Technical Readiness Bar](journeys/ESTABLISH-AI-TECHNICAL-READINESS-BAR.md) when the CTO or equivalent technology leader needs consistent standards for platform dependencies, context, data, evaluation, controls, and incident readiness.

**Leadership result:** a reusable technical readiness standard with evidence thresholds, accountable owners, escalation conditions, and initiative-level exceptions made visible.

### Start with an organizational assessment

Choose [Assess an Organization](journeys/ASSESS-AN-ORGANIZATION.md) when disconnected initiatives, inconsistent practices, or unclear ownership span multiple teams or categories.

**Leadership result:** an evidence-based category profile, the weakest consequential conditions, prioritized interventions, accountable owners, and a dated operating-model roadmap.

### Start with an initiative decision

Choose [Evaluate an AI Initiative](journeys/EVALUATE-AN-AI-INITIATIVE.md) when a specific opportunity, pilot, funding, launch, scale, sensitive-data, or autonomy decision is pending.

**Leadership result:** a proceed, proceed-with-constraints, remediate-and-re-review, pause, or stop recommendation with evidence conditions, ownership, expiry, and the next review trigger.

### Start with a product-team workflow

Choose [Redesign a Product-Team Workflow](journeys/REDESIGN-A-PRODUCT-TEAM-WORKFLOW.md) when recurring work is slow, inconsistent, context-poor, or being changed through AI assistance.

**Leadership result:** an observed baseline, explicit human-AI responsibilities, a bounded pilot, measures and countermeasures, and a reusable playbook only when representative evidence supports one.

If the issue is unclear, use the product-direction path when strategic choices are unsettled, the technical-readiness path when teams lack a shared engineering bar, the organization path when the issue spans several teams, the initiative path when a material bet is pending, and the workflow path when the unit of change is recurring product-team work.

## A Practical First 90 Days

This is an adoption route, not a claim that every organization should move at the same pace.

### Days 0–30: Orient and choose

- Name one consequential organizational, portfolio, initiative, or workflow decision.
- Identify the accountable executive and the people who hold relevant operational, customer, technical, data, legal, privacy, security, safety, or risk evidence.
- Establish what evidence already exists, what is assumed, and which critical gaps cannot be averaged away.
- Choose the smallest AIPOM starting path capable of improving the decision.

**Exit condition:** a bounded decision, named owner, relevant participants, known evidence, explicit assumptions, and an agreed review point.

### Days 31–60: Run and test

- Facilitate the selected path using real organizational evidence where it can be shared appropriately.
- Make human decision authority, AI responsibilities, controls, escalation, and fallback explicit.
- Produce the decision or operating artifact promised by the path.
- Test the recommendation or redesigned practice at bounded scope before standardizing it.

**Exit condition:** a decision has been clarified, constrained, changed, or supported; unresolved questions and evidence conditions are visible; next actions have owners and dates.

### Days 61–90: Review and institutionalize selectively

- Review what happened in use, including friction, exceptions, failures, burden, and unintended consequences.
- Decide whether to adopt, revise, repeat, constrain, or retire the practice.
- Convert a local practice into a reusable playbook or skill only when representative use supports it.
- Select the next consequential condition based on evidence, not catalog coverage.

**Exit condition:** leadership has an evidence-based adoption decision, an owner for stewardship, and a next review trigger. Completion of an artifact alone is not sufficient.

## What Evidence of Progress Looks Like

Weak signals include tools purchased, logins, prompts submitted, pilots announced, documents generated, workshops completed, and policies published.

Stronger evidence includes:

- A funding, launch, scale, constraint, or stop decision changed because relevant evidence became visible
- Learning or time-to-decision improved without shifting hidden review burden to people
- Rework, failure exposure, or avoidable risk decreased
- Customer, business, safety, compliance, or operational outcomes improved
- Teams consistently use a practice with named ownership and review
- An effective practice is reused and improved across representative settings

AIPOM distinguishes a practice being discussed, documented, consistently used, and demonstrably improving decisions or outcomes. Without credible evidence, maturity should not be scored above `2 — Emerging`.

## Leadership Responsibilities

AI may assist, analyze, simulate, draft, recommend, and perform bounded actions. It does not absorb human accountability.

Leaders remain responsible for ensuring that:

- Accountable decision owners are named
- Specialist approval is obtained where required
- Authority and autonomy boundaries match the consequences
- Critical gaps are not hidden inside an average score or optimistic business case
- Evidence claims are proportionate to what has actually been observed
- Escalation, incident response, recovery, and review triggers are usable
- Teams can stop activity that creates motion without sufficient value or control

## What AIPOM Does Not Claim

AIPOM is not a prompt library, vendor comparison, AI tool tutorial, machine-learning engineering handbook, legal opinion, or substitute for accountable domain review. A completed assessment, policy, workshop, or skill does not establish compliance, adoption, maturity, or impact.

The `v0.6` public preview is structurally validated and synthetically forward-tested, including clean-room cold-start checks for all five Starter Packs. It has not yet established representative field effectiveness or demonstrated organizational adoption and impact. See the [Public Preview Contract](PUBLIC-PREVIEW.md) and [Evidence Status](EVIDENCE-STATUS.md).

## The Executive Commitment

The smallest credible commitment is not “adopt AIPOM.” It is:

> Select one consequential AI product decision, bring the relevant evidence and accountable people together, use a bounded path to improve that decision, and review what changed before expanding the practice.

That creates a practical route from scattered activity toward a repeatable operating system without pretending that framework adoption is the outcome.
