# Evidence Standard

This document defines how AIPOM distinguishes organizational claims from demonstrated practice.

## Four Levels of Claim

1. **Discussed:** people talk about the practice or intend to use it.
2. **Documented:** a policy, process, template, or decision describes the practice.
3. **Used:** examples show the practice being applied consistently in real work.
4. **Impact demonstrated:** measures or decision records show the practice improving decisions or outcomes.

These levels are cumulative. A document does not prove use, and use does not prove impact.

## Evidence Types

Useful evidence may include:

- Existing artifacts with dates and owners
- Examples of actual use across more than one case
- Decision records and changed decisions
- Evaluation results and production observations
- Customer or end-user research
- Adoption evidence tied to changed behavior
- Business, operational, safety, compliance, or risk measures
- Escalations, incidents, rollbacks, and corrective actions
- Evidence that a practice is reused and improved

Policies, licenses, tool deployment, training attendance, workshop completion, prompts submitted, tokens consumed, logins, pilots launched, or documents generated are not sufficient evidence of maturity by themselves.

## Evidence Quality Dimensions

Evaluate evidence across five dimensions:

| Dimension | Question |
|---|---|
| Relevance | Does it directly support the condition or claim being assessed? |
| Reliability | Is its source credible, inspectable, and appropriately independent? |
| Recency | Is it current enough for the decision? |
| Representativeness | Does it cover meaningful teams, users, cases, and failure modes? |
| Consequence | Does it show a decision, behavior, or outcome changed? |

Evidence can be useful while still incomplete. Record limitations rather than discarding or overstating it.

## Evidence Ledger

For consequential findings, record:

| Field | Description |
|---|---|
| Claim | What is believed to be true |
| Operating level | Organization, portfolio, product team, or initiative |
| Evidence | What can be inspected |
| Source and owner | Where it came from and who is responsible for it |
| Date or period | When it was created or observed |
| Quality limitations | Missing perspectives, weak samples, staleness, conflicts, or bias |
| Confidence | High, medium, or low, with a short rationale |
| Decision implication | What the evidence supports doing or not doing |
| Next evidence | What would materially increase or reduce confidence |

## Assumptions

When evidence is unavailable:

- State what is missing.
- Label the assumption.
- Explain its consequence if wrong.
- Do not award a maturity score above `2 — Emerging`.
- Recommend a proportionate way to obtain evidence.

Lack of evidence is not automatically evidence that a practice is absent. It is evidence that the maturity claim is not yet demonstrated.

## Conflicting Evidence

Do not flatten disagreement among executives, teams, governance partners, or users.

Record:

- The competing claims
- Evidence supporting each claim
- Differences in operating level or scope
- Missing perspectives
- Why the disagreement matters
- The next action that could resolve it

## Critical Gaps

A high average cannot compensate for a missing non-negotiable control involving safety, legal obligations, privacy, security, decision authority, or accountability.

Critical gaps should be reported separately and may constrain a recommendation to launch, scale, automate, or continue.

## Outcome Evidence

Prefer evidence of:

- Better or faster decisions
- Shorter learning cycles
- Reduced rework
- Improved customer outcomes
- Increased revenue or protected margin
- Reduced churn or operating cost
- Lower risk
- Improved safety or compliance performance
- Reuse and improvement of effective practices

AI activity is not evidence of AI capability.
